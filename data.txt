What is Artificial Intelligence (AI)?|AI is a branch of science that deals with helping machines find solutions to complex problems in a human-like fashion, borrowing characteristics from human intelligence and applying them as algorithms.
When was AI formally initiated?|AI was formally initiated in 1956, when the name was coined, although work had been under way for about five years prior.
What are the four possible goals of AI?|Systems that think like humans, systems that act like humans, systems that think rationally, and systems that act rationally.
What is the Turing Test?|A test proposed by Alan Turing where a computer is interrogated by a human via text; the computer passes if the interrogator cannot tell whether it is a human or a machine at the other end.
What capabilities does a computer need to pass the Turing Test?|Natural language processing, knowledge representation, automated reasoning, and machine learning.
What additional capabilities are needed to pass the Total Turing Test?|Computer vision (to perceive objects) and robotics (to move them about).
What is the cognitive modelling approach in AI?|An approach that tries to make programs think like humans by modelling human mental processes through introspection and psychological experiments.
Who developed GPS (General Problem Solver) and what was special about it?|Newell and Simon developed GPS; they were concerned with comparing the program's reasoning steps to human subjects solving the same problems.
What is the laws-of-thought (logicist) approach in AI?|An approach based on formal logic aimed at building intelligent systems that use logical notation to find solutions to problems.
What is the rational agent approach in AI?|Acting rationally means acting to achieve one's goals given one's beliefs; AI is viewed as the study and construction of rational agents.
What is Narrow AI (Weak AI)?|AI designed to perform a specific narrow task (e.g., facial recognition, internet search, chess); it operates within a limited pre-defined range of contexts.
What is General AI (Strong AI)?|AI endowed with broad human-like cognitive capabilities, able to tackle new and unfamiliar tasks autonomously without human guidance.
What is Superintelligent AI?|A speculative future form of AI where machines could surpass human intelligence across all fields including creativity and problem-solving.
What are Reactive Machines in AI?|AI systems that do not store memories or past experiences; they only analyze and respond to current situations. Example: IBM's Deep Blue.
What is Limited Memory AI?|AI systems that can make improved decisions by studying past data they have collected. Most current AI (chatbots, self-driving cars) falls into this category.
What is Theory of Mind AI?|An advanced type of AI (still being researched) that would understand and remember emotions, beliefs, and needs to make decisions based on them.
What is Self-Aware AI?|A theoretical future AI with its own consciousness, sentience, and self-awareness, capable of understanding and possessing emotions.
What is Machine Learning (ML)?|A type of AI capable of self-improvement through experience without direct programming; it creates software that learns independently by accessing and using data.
What is Deep Learning?|A subset of ML involving many layers of neural networks; used for learning from large amounts of data, powering voice control, image recognition, and more.
What is Natural Language Processing (NLP)?|An AI technology that enables machines to understand and interpret human language; used in chatbots, translation services, and sentiment analysis.
What is Computer Vision?|An AI technology that allows machines to interpret the world visually; used in medical image analysis, surveillance, and autonomous vehicles.
What are Expert Systems?|AI systems that answer questions and solve problems in a specific domain of expertise using rule-based systems, mimicking human expert reasoning.
What is the primary goal of AI?|To simulate human-like intelligence in machines, empowering them to carry out complex tasks and decision-making processes autonomously.
What are the main branches of AI?|Machine learning, deep learning, natural language processing, robotics, and expert systems.
How does AI apply to e-commerce?|AI powers recommendation engines that analyze customer behavior to suggest products and chatbots that provide customer support.
How does AI apply to healthcare?|AI algorithms analyze medical images, predict disease outcomes, assist in drug discovery, and develop personalized treatment plans.
How does AI apply to navigation?|AI processes real-time traffic data, weather conditions, and historical patterns to provide accurate route suggestions (e.g., Google Maps).
What are the advantages of AI?|Reduction in human error, enhanced decision-making, zero risk in dangerous tasks, 24/7 availability, digital assistance, new inventions, unbiased decisions, and automation.
What are the disadvantages of AI?|Lack of creativity, lack of emotional intelligence, encourages human laziness, raises privacy concerns, leads to job displacement, over-dependence on technology, algorithm development concerns, environmental issues, and lack of common sense.
What is a state in AI problem-solving?|A state represents the status of the solution at a given step of the problem-solving procedure.
What is state space search?|A method of solving problems where operators are applied to states to generate new states, continuing until the goal state is reached.
What is a production system in AI?|A system consisting of a set of IF-THEN rules, one or more knowledge bases, a control strategy, and a rule applier used to solve problems.
What is the water jug problem in AI?|A classic AI problem where you must get exactly 2 gallons in a 4-gallon jug using a 3-gallon jug; solved using state space search and production rules.
What is breadth-first search (BFS)?|A search technique where the children (neighbors) of a node are visited before grandchildren; it explores level by level. Time and space complexity: O(b^(d+1)).
What is depth-first search (DFS)?|A search technique that follows a path to its end before exploring another path. It has low storage requirements but may find sub-optimal solutions.
What is bounded depth-first search?|A variation of DFS with a maximum depth limit; beyond the depth bound, failure is generated automatically.
What is iterative deepening search?|A search that begins with depth bound 1 and increases by 1 until a solution is found; finds optimal solutions with low storage requirements.
What is a heuristic search?|A search method that uses domain knowledge to choose more promising paths first; it may not always find the best solution but finds a good one in reasonable time.
What is the Travelling Salesman Problem?|A problem where a salesperson must visit each city exactly once and find the shortest route; it illustrates combinatorial explosion and the need for heuristic search.
What is Knowledge Representation (KR)?|An area of AI concerned with how to formally represent knowledge so that computers can use it for reasoning and problem-solving.
What is a Representation and Reasoning System (RRS)?|A system composed of a language to communicate with a computer, a way to assign meaning to the language, and procedures to compute answers.
What are the four properties of a good knowledge representation system?|Representational adequacy, inferential adequacy, inferential efficiency, and acquisitional efficiency.
What is simple relational knowledge representation?|The simplest way to store facts by setting each fact about a set of objects out systematically in columns; offers little opportunity for inference.
What is inheritable knowledge representation?|An extension of relational knowledge that allows inference through property inheritance; elements inherit values from being members of a class.
What is inferential knowledge representation?|Representing knowledge as formal logic (e.g., ∀x: dog(x) → hasatail(x)), allowing derivation of new facts with guaranteed correctness.
What is procedural knowledge representation?|Knowledge encoded in procedures (small programs) that know how to do specific things; allows heuristic reasoning but may sacrifice completeness and modularity.
What is a semantic network?|A labeled graph for knowledge representation where the meaning of a concept comes from its relationship to other concepts; nodes are concepts and labeled arcs are relationships.
What are the two main inference methods in a semantic network?|Intersection search (spreading activation from two nodes to find their intersection) and inheritance (using isa and instance links to propagate properties).
What is a frame in AI?|A collection of attributes (slots) and associated values that describe some real-world entity; used for structured knowledge representation similar to objects in OOP.
What is a metaclass in frame systems?|A special class whose elements are themselves classes; used to resolve the catch-22 between treating an entity as both a class and an instance.
What is an expert system?|A computer program built with human expert assistance that functions at the standard of a human expert in a given field.
What are the three essential components of a rule-based expert system?|The knowledge base (stores domain knowledge as facts and rules), the working memory (stores facts produced during processing), and the inference engine (applies rules to produce new facts).
What is forward chaining in expert systems?|A data-driven inference method that starts with known facts and applies rules to derive new conclusions until no more rules apply or the goal is reached.
What is backward chaining in expert systems?|A goal-driven inference method that starts with a hypothesis/goal and works backward to find supporting facts.
What is forward chaining best used for?|When you have a small set of initial facts and want to find all possible conclusions, or when many rules could derive the same conclusion.
What is backward chaining best used for?|When trying to prove a single specific hypothesis given a large set of initial facts.
What are common conflict resolution strategies in rule-based systems?|First Applicable, Random, Most Specific, Least Recently Used, and Best Rule (weight-based).
What is Bayesian probability in AI?|A technique based on conditional probability (P(B/A) = P(A/B)*P(B)/P(A)) used to handle uncertainty in rule-based reasoning.
What is a certainty factor (CF) in fuzzy logic?|A number in the interval [-1, 1] indicating the degree of certainty of a conclusion; CF=1 means certainly true, CF=-1 means certainly false.
What is the CF of a conjunction of facts?|The minimum CF among the CFs of each individual fact: CF(A AND B AND C) = min(CF(A), CF(B), CF(C)).
What is the CF of a disjunction of facts?|The maximum CF among the CFs of each individual fact: CF(A OR B OR C) = max(CF(A), CF(B), CF(C)).
What are the limitations of rule-based expert systems?|Knowledge acquisition is difficult, only feasible when all knowledge can be expressed as IF-THEN rules, and performance degrades with too many rules.
What is Case-Based Reasoning (CBR)?|A reasoning approach where expertise is embodied in a library of past cases rather than rules; current problems are solved by retrieving and adapting similar past cases.
What are the four steps of the CBR cycle?|Retrieve the most similar case, reuse the retrieved case to solve the current problem, revise the solution if necessary, retain the final solution as a new case.
What is Natural Language Processing in AI?|The field of AI that deals with enabling computers to process, understand, and generate human language.
What is a context-free grammar (CFG)?|A grammar consisting of rules for replacing non-terminal symbols with sequences of terminals and non-terminals, used to describe sentence structure.
What is a parse tree?|A tree structure that records how a sentence is composed of phrases (noun phrases, verb phrases, etc.) according to grammar rules.
What is a transition net (parser)?|An alternative to CFG-based parsing that uses nodes and directed arcs to traverse sentence structure; creates parse tree nodes and attaches them when arcs are traversed.
What is pattern recognition in AI?|The act of taking in raw data and classifying it based on a priori knowledge or statistical information extracted from the patterns.
What is the training set in pattern recognition?|A set of patterns that have already been classified, used as the basis for supervised learning in a pattern recognition system.
What are the two main approaches to pattern recognition?|Statistical (decision theoretic) pattern recognition and syntactical (structural) pattern recognition.
What is a Genetic Algorithm (GA)?|A search technique inspired by evolutionary biology (inheritance, mutation, selection, crossover) used to find approximate solutions to optimization problems.
What are the four main operators in a Genetic Algorithm?|Selection (choosing fit individuals), crossover/recombination (breeding new solutions), mutation (random changes), and inversion.
What are the termination conditions for a Genetic Algorithm?|A satisfactory solution is found, a fixed number of generations is reached, the allocated budget is exhausted, or fitness has plateaued.
What is an Artificial Neural Network (ANN)?|A system based on biological neural networks consisting of interconnected processing units (neurons) that learn to perform functions from data.
What are the three types of units in a neural network?|Input units (receive data from outside), output units (send data out), and hidden units (internal processing).
What are the three types of activation functions in neural networks?|Threshold function, piecewise-linear function, and sigmoid function.
What is supervised learning in neural networks?|Training where the network is provided with input and matching output patterns (teacher-provided) and adjusts weights to minimize error.
What is unsupervised learning in neural networks?|Training where the network discovers statistical regularities in input data without any pre-labeled categories.
What is reinforcement learning in neural networks?|Learning where the system performs actions on an environment, receives feedback (reward or punishment), and adjusts parameters accordingly.
What is a Feedforward/MLP neural network?|A network where data flows strictly from input to hidden layers to output with no feedback connections; good for tabular data and basic classification.
What is a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)?|A neural network using convolution and pooling layers that exploits local patterns and spatial structure; used for image classification and object detection.
What is a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN)?|A neural network containing loops where output at time t feeds into computation at t+1; effective for sequences like text, speech, and time series.
What is a Transformer network?|A neural network built from self-attention and feedforward blocks instead of recurrence; the state-of-the-art for large language models (e.g., GPT).
What is a Generative Adversarial Network (GAN)?|A network consisting of a Generator (creates realistic samples) and a Discriminator (distinguishes real from fake) trained in an adversarial game.
What is a Graph Neural Network (GNN)?|A neural network suited for graph-structured data where node embeddings are updated by aggregating information from neighbors; used for social networks and molecules.
What is a state space in AI?|An abstract space containing every possible condition (state) of a system; a problem is modeled as a search through the state space from initial state to goal state.
What is a Bayesian Network?|A directed acyclic graph where nodes are random variables and edges represent direct probabilistic dependence; the joint distribution factorizes as a product of conditional probabilities.
What is a Markov Random Field (MRF)?|An undirected probabilistic graphical model where a node is independent of non-neighbors given its neighbors (Markov property); used for spatial data like images.
What is deductive reasoning in KR?|Moving from general statements (premises) to specific conclusions that logically follow (top-down logic).
What is inductive reasoning in KR?|Forming general conclusions from specific observations; conclusions are probable but not guaranteed (bottom-up logic).
What is abductive reasoning in KR?|Inference to the best explanation — forming the most plausible hypothesis that explains a set of observations.
What is the frame problem in AI?|The problem of representing what does not change when an action occurs without specifying an enormous number of "frame axioms."
What is non-monotonic reasoning?|Reasoning where adding new facts can invalidate old conclusions, unlike classical logic where additional facts cannot invalidate existing conclusions.
What is default logic?|A non-monotonic logic that allows default rules of the form "if A is believed and B is consistent, then conclude C"; allows reasoning with typical assumptions.
What is propositional logic?|A branch of formal logic that studies logical relationships between propositions (true/false statements) combined using logical connectives.
What is First-Order Logic (FOL)?|An extension of propositional logic that allows reasoning about objects, properties, and relationships using predicates, variables, and quantifiers (∀ and ∃).
What is a Knowledge Graph (KG)?|A structured representation of entities and their relationships as triples (subject, predicate, object); widely used in search engines and question-answering systems.
What is the formal definition of a knowledge graph triple?|A triple (h, r, t) where h is the head entity, r is the relation, and t is the tail entity; represented as a directed labeled edge h →r t.
What is SPARQL?|A declarative query language for RDF knowledge graphs, analogous to SQL for relational databases.
What are the challenges of knowledge graphs?|Scalability over billions of triples, data quality (noise and contradictions), ontology engineering, and integration with machine learning.
What are the characteristics of an expert system?|Expert-level performance, knowledge-based operation, inference capability, explanation facility, reliability, domain-specificity, and heuristic reasoning.
What are the five components of an expert system?|Knowledge base, inference engine, user interface, explanation system, and knowledge acquisition module.
What are the types of expert systems?|Rule-based, frame-based, fuzzy, neural, and hybrid expert systems.
What are the steps in expert system development?|Problem identification, knowledge acquisition, knowledge representation, system design, implementation, testing & validation, and deployment & maintenance.
What programming language dominates AI development and why?|Python dominates due to its simple readable syntax, massive ecosystem for ML/DL/NLP, and strong community support.
What is Python best suited for in AI?|Machine learning, deep learning, research and prototyping, and production AI systems.
What is C++ used for in AI?|Performance-critical AI components and real-time AI such as robotics, game AI, and embedded systems.
What is Julia used for in AI?|Research-focused AI and high-performance mathematical models; it combines Python-like syntax with near C-level speed.
What is Computer Image Recognition?|A field of AI that enables computers to identify, analyze, and understand objects, patterns, or features in digital images.
What are the steps in the image recognition process?|Image acquisition, image preprocessing, feature extraction, and classification/recognition.
What are the three main techniques used in image recognition?|Traditional methods (edge detection, template matching), machine learning methods (KNN, SVM, Decision Trees), and deep learning methods (CNN, DNN).
What are the advantages of Computer Image Recognition?|Fast and accurate analysis, reduces human effort, works continuously without fatigue, and useful in dangerous environments.
What are the limitations of Computer Image Recognition?|Requires large datasets, sensitive to image quality, high computational cost, and affected by lighting and angle variations.
What is a digital image made of?|Small elements called pixels, where each pixel represents color or intensity; images are represented as matrices of pixel values.
